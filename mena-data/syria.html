Background and Context

Syria's crisis began in March 2011 when peaceful protests in the southern city of Daraa were met with a violent government crackdown under President Bashar Al-Assad. Demonstrations spread rapidly across the country. The government's response escalated from mass arrests and torture to the deployment of military force against civilian populations.

The conflict rapidly militarized, drawing in a complex array of armed actors: the Syrian military, Iranian-backed militias, Russian forces (from 2015), various armed opposition factions, Kurdish forces (primarily the SDF in the northeast), and the Islamic State (ISIS/ISIL), which at its peak in 2014–2015 controlled large portions of eastern Syria and western Iraq.

The Assad government's military strategy included the documented deliberate targeting of civilian infrastructure as a tactic of war — including hospitals, schools, water treatment plants, markets, and bakeries. The UN Commission of Inquiry on Syria documented widespread and systematic human rights violations, including the use of chemical weapons confirmed by the OPCW, forced disappearances, torture in detention, and siege warfare. At the crisis's peak, over 13 million Syrians were displaced.

On December 8, 2024, opposition forces led primarily by Hayat Tahrir al-Sham (HTS) swept through Syrian territory at unexpected speed. The Assad government collapsed and Bashar Al-Assad fled to Russia, ending over 54 years of Assad family rule. Ahmad Al Sharaa was appointed as president for a transitional period beginning January 2025. The fall of the regime triggered an immediate wave of refugee and IDP returns: by mid-2025, over 1.2 million Syrian refugees had returned from Turkey, Lebanon, and Jordan, and 1.9 million IDPs had gone back to their areas of origin.

Current Humanitarian Conditions (2025)

Despite the historic political transition, humanitarian needs in Syria remain at record levels. The UN-coordinated Syria Humanitarian Response Plan for 2025 targets 16.5 million people — essentially the same number as in recent years — reflecting the deep structural nature of the crisis. Political change has not immediately translated into improved conditions on the ground.

Communities receiving returnees face acute strain. Housing stock across formerly conflict-affected areas is decimated — in many areas, returnees are living in rubble or partially-habitable structures without electricity, running water, or sanitation. Landmines and unexploded ordnance pose a serious and ongoing threat: since December 2024 alone, at least 317 people have been killed in ordnance accidents, including 71 children. Syria is among the most landmine-contaminated countries in the world.

New violence has periodically disrupted the transition. In August 2025, clashes erupted in Sweida governorate in southern Syria, displacing approximately 190,000 people. Wildfires in Latakia and Tartus provinces affected thousands more. Security incidents continue in the northeast, and the political and military status of various armed factions across the country remains unresolved.

An estimated 38% of Syria's hospitals are non-functional or only partially operational. Fourteen years of conflict have left more than 7.8 million children in need of educational support. Water infrastructure, energy systems, and roads require massive investment before sustainable returns can occur at scale.

Humanitarian Needs by Sector

Food Security

14.56 million Syrians face food insecurity — one of the largest food crises in the world. Poverty, unemployment, currency collapse, and degraded agricultural systems have driven chronic hunger across most governorates. WFP emergency food assistance reaches millions monthly, but funding gaps routinely force program reductions.

Assessment: Very Severe

Healthcare

38% of health facilities are non- or partially functional after years of deliberate targeting and deterioration. Shortages of medicines, trained personnel, and medical equipment are widespread. Trauma care capacity, vaccination coverage, and mental health services all fall far below the needs of the population.

Assessment: Severe

Shelter and Housing

Massive housing stock destruction exists across formerly conflict-affected areas, especially Aleppo, Raqqa, Deir ez-Zor, Homs, and Daraa. Returnees frequently find their homes uninhabitable or occupied. Overcrowded informal settlements and camps continue to house hundreds of thousands with no durable solution in sight.

Assessment: Severe

Education

7.8 million+ children require educational support. Schools have been destroyed, repurposed, or left without teachers and supplies across conflict-affected areas. An estimated one-third of school-age children are not enrolled. The long-term human capital cost of the education crisis is immense.

Assessment: Serious

Mine Action and Unexploded Ordnance

Syria is one of the most heavily contaminated countries in the world from landmines, cluster munitions, and improvised explosive devices. Since December 2024 alone, 317 people have been killed in ordnance incidents, including 71 children. Contamination blocks agricultural land, roads, and residential areas from safe use by returnees.

Assessment: Critical for returns

Crisis Timeline

  1. March 2011 — Protests Begin in Daraa

    Peaceful pro-democracy protests are met with violent government crackdown. Demonstrations spread across Syria, marking the beginning of the civil war.

  2. 2012–2013 — Militarization and Sieges

    The conflict escalates into a full civil war. Government forces besiege cities including Homs, Aleppo, and Eastern Ghouta, cutting off food, medicine, and water from civilian populations.

  3. 2013 — Chemical Weapons Attacks Confirmed

    UN investigators confirm the use of chemical weapons by Syrian government forces, including a sarin attack on Ghouta killing over 1,400 people — one of the deadliest chemical weapons incidents since World War I.

  4. 2014–2015 — Rise of ISIS and Peak Displacement

    ISIS takes control of large parts of eastern Syria and western Iraq. Syria's displacement crisis reaches its peak. Over 4 million Syrians flee the country, triggering a large-scale refugee movement into Europe and neighboring countries.

  5. September 2015 — Russian Military Intervention

    Russia begins military operations in support of the Assad government, conducting widespread airstrikes. The military balance shifts decisively, enabling government forces to retake opposition-held territory over the following years.

  6. February 2023 — Catastrophic Earthquake

    A 7.8-magnitude earthquake strikes southern Turkey and northern Syria, killing over 50,000 people across both countries and devastating already-damaged cities including Aleppo, Latakia, and Hama.

  7. December 8, 2024 — Fall of the Assad Regime

    Opposition forces capture Damascus in a rapid offensive. Bashar Al-Assad flees to Russia after 54 years of Assad family rule. Ahmad Al Sharaa appointed transitional president.

  8. 2025 — Mass Returns and Ongoing Crisis

    1.2 million refugees and 1.9 million IDPs return to Syria. Humanitarian needs remain at record levels. Violence persists in multiple regions. Landmine casualties mount as returnees re-enter contaminated areas.

Aid Response and Funding

The 2025 Syria Humanitarian Response Plan requests approximately $4.07 billion to serve 16.5 million people across all sectors. Historically, Syria appeals have been funded at roughly 50–65% of requested levels — leaving critical gaps in food assistance, healthcare, shelter, and education. Major donors include the United States, European Union member states, and Gulf Cooperation Council countries.
